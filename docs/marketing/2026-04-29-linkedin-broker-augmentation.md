## Body — English

After 4 years selling US liquidation truckloads to importers across LATAM, I learned something the fintech blogs don't tell you:

The smartest thing you can sell to the Miami trade community isn't a tool that **replaces** the customs broker.

It's a tool that makes the broker's day faster.

Brokers in Doral and at the US-Mexico border crossings spend roughly 40% of their working hours on routine document review and HS code lookups. Multiply that by 20–50 active client files per broker, every week, and you have an entire industry of licensed professionals stuck doing the work that AI can do in 15 seconds.

So we built Puente AI for both sides of that table.

For the SME — the Maria of the corridor, importing or exporting: upload your invoice, get compliance gaps and a payment-route recommendation in 15 seconds. Today the route is a recommendation; the math is honest about what each option costs and what each option saves.

For the customs broker — the Carlos of the corridor: the same engine, white-labeled into your book of clients. The routine 40% becomes one screen. The complex 60% — the cases that earn your fee — gets all of your attention.

We are broker-augmentation, not broker-replacement.

If you're a Miami-based SME or a licensed US customs broker working the US–LATAM corridor, I'd like 30 minutes of your time this month. No demo deck. No pitch. Just questions about what slows your week down and what a 15-second answer would change.

DM in English or Spanish.

— Jay

## Notes for the founder (READ BEFORE PUBLISHING)

1. **Headcount placeholder:** The post does not include any "[N] users" or "[N] brokers" claim, which is correct because there are no signed pilot customers yet. Do **not** add such a claim before publishing — wait until KAN-22 customer interviews convert at least one paying pilot.

2. **40% claim:** "Brokers spend roughly 40% of their working hours on routine document review and HS code lookups." This figure is sourced from the PRD (`docs/PRD.md` §4 Carlos persona description). It is an internal estimate, not a published industry benchmark. Keep the word **roughly** in the EN version and **alrededor del** in the ES version — both qualifiers are doing real work. If asked in the comments where the number comes from, the honest answer is *"that's our internal estimate from founder conversations and the PRD persona work; we'd love a more rigorous benchmark and would welcome a broker telling us we have it wrong."*

3. **Money-movement language:** The EN body says "the math is honest about what each option costs and what each option saves" — and the ES says "la matemática es honesta sobre lo que cada opción cuesta y lo que cada opción ahorra." Both are deliberately phrased to describe the *recommendation*, not execution. Do not edit either to say "we save you money" or "we move your money for less" — both would imply Puente executes settlement, which V1 does not. (Compliance guardrail #1 in `.claude/agents/marketing-pr.md`.)

4. **Spanish vocabulary check (Miami-specific):** I used "agente de aduana" as the Spanish term for customs broker — most universal across LATAM written usage. In Miami the spoken term in mixed-language conversation is often "broker aduanero" or just "broker" (Spanglish). Before publishing, consider whether to swap "agente de aduana" → "broker aduanero" for the ES post specifically. The word "broker" itself works in both languages and might land better with the Doral audience. Recommendation: keep "agente de aduana" for the LinkedIn ES version (more formal register, broker-LinkedIn audience leans formal) but use "broker" for the WhatsApp version (informal register).

5. **Spanish formality register:** I used *tú* (informal) throughout. This matches the founder-voice / WhatsApp-channel guidance in the agent prompt. For the LinkedIn ES version specifically, consider whether the broker audience expects *usted* (more formal) — if the founder's existing LinkedIn presence already uses *tú* with this community, keep it; if not, request a *usted* rewrite before publishing.

6. **30-minute discovery call ask:** The CTA "30 minutes of your time this month" is honest about the time commitment and matches KAN-22 scope. Before publishing, confirm there is a calendar booking link (Calendly / Cal.com / native LinkedIn meeting scheduler) to point repliers at — otherwise the DM-to-call conversion drops sharply.

7. **No partisan tariff language.** The post stays clear of Trump-tariff or political framing per compliance guardrail #6, even though tariff volatility is the live KAN-18 wedge. Keep it that way.

8. **Visibility timing:** Publish the EN version on a Tuesday or Wednesday morning Miami time (highest LinkedIn engagement for B2B fintech / trade audiences). ES version 24 hours later, same window.
